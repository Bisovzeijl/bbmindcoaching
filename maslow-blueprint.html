Most people assume their struggles are about motivation, discipline, or mindset. Usually they are not. They are structural. This tool shows you where.

What this tool is

A 30-question diagnostic built on Abraham Maslow's hierarchy of needs. Not a quiz. Not a personality test. A structural analysis of which levels of your psychological foundation are solid, which are fragile, and which are silently undermining everything above them.

30

6 per level, each tagged to a specific dimension so you know exactly which aspect of each level is the problem.

Blueprint Types

Levels Mapped

Physiological, Safety, Belonging, Esteem, and Self-Actualisation. Each scored out of 30 and interpreted individually.

Pattern Alert

The tool detects the "false peak" pattern: upper levels appearing strong while lower levels are quietly failing. No other tool surfaces this.

How it works

  • 1
    Take the assessment 30 slider questions. Each takes about 10 seconds. Total time: 8 to 12 minutes. Answer honestly. The tool cannot help you if you answer the way you wish things were.
  • 2
    Receive your Blueprint Profile Your scores across all 5 levels, your Blueprint Type with a personalised explanation, level-by-level insights, and the Structural Pattern Alert if it applies to you.
  • 3
    Review your Action Plan Each level receives a priority label and three specific, non-generic actions matched to your exact score band. Not generic advice. Actions calibrated to where you actually are.
  • 4
    Read the Full Framework A deep-dive into the theory behind every level, including what unmet needs look like in real life, and an honest note on what the model gets wrong. Understanding the framework makes the actions land differently.

An honest note before you begin. Maslow's hierarchy is a model, not a law. It is a map, and like all maps it is a simplification of the territory. This tool will give you useful information. It will not give you the complete picture of who you are. Use it as a starting point for honest reflection, not as a verdict.

The five levels

Physiological Sleep, energy, physical health, nutrition, and rest. The base everything else is built on.
Safety Financial stability, predictability, structure, and freedom from chronic threat.
Belonging Deep connection, being known, community, and the feeling of mattering to others.
Esteem Self-respect, competence, recognition, and the internal sense of being capable.
Self-Actualisation Purpose, growth, creative expression, and becoming the person you are capable of being.

Based on the work of Abraham Maslow, "A Theory of Human Motivation" (1943)

The Full Framework

Understanding the theory behind the tool makes the actions land differently. This is not background reading. It is part of the tool.

01 Physiological Needs

Maslow placed physiological needs at the base of the hierarchy because they are the literal preconditions for everything else. When the body's core needs are unmet, the brain's threat-detection system stays activated, making sustained focus, emotional regulation, and complex decision-making genuinely harder to access. This is not a metaphor. It is neuroscience.

The modern version of physiological deprivation is rarely dramatic. It looks like chronic low-grade sleep debt. A schedule that leaves no room for physical recovery. Energy that runs on caffeine and willpower rather than genuine restoration. People in this pattern often perform at a high level for years before the foundation cracks.

What unmet physiological needs look like in real life

Waking tired every morning and accepting it as normal. Using exercise as punishment rather than care. Meals as something to get through rather than something to be present for. A baseline level of physical tension that has been there so long it no longer registers as unusual.

The key insight: physiological needs are not the "easy" level. They are the level most systematically ignored by high-achieving people who have learned to override physical signals in service of productivity. The body keeps score. The question is when you choose to read it.

02 Safety Needs

Safety needs are about predictability, not the absence of risk. The brain is not looking for a life with no challenges. It is looking for a life where the rules are stable enough to plan within. Chronic unpredictability, whether financial, relational, or environmental, keeps the nervous system in a low-grade vigilance state that consumes cognitive resources constantly.

Financial insecurity is the most common safety need failure in the adults this tool is designed for. Not poverty, but the specific anxiety of insufficient margin: the background hum of "what if" that makes it hard to think beyond the next crisis. Structural instability in relationships or living situations creates a similar effect through different mechanisms.

What unmet safety needs look like in real life

Difficulty thinking long-term because the short-term feels uncertain. Overworking as a response to financial anxiety rather than genuine ambition. Difficulty relaxing even when circumstances allow it. A persistent low-level sense that something could go wrong at any moment.

The key insight: safety needs are not about wealth. They are about margin. A person with a modest income and strong financial buffers has more safety need satisfaction than a high earner who spends everything and carries chronic debt anxiety. Structure creates safety. Certainty is built, not found.

03 Belonging Needs

Belonging is the most frequently underestimated level for high-performing adults. It is easy to misread. A full social calendar is not belonging. A large network is not belonging. Being liked is not belonging. Belonging is specifically the experience of being known, of having people in your life who see you clearly and remain present anyway.

The distinction that matters here is between surface connection and deep connection. Many striving professionals have extensive shallow networks and almost no deep ones. They are surrounded by people and functionally isolated. This gap produces a specific kind of loneliness that is difficult to name precisely because it contradicts the external evidence.

What unmet belonging needs look like in real life

Performing a version of yourself at work and feeling vaguely exhausted by it. Struggling to ask for help because you are not sure how it would be received. Achieving things and finding the satisfaction short-lived because there is nobody to be fully honest with about the cost. Feeling like you would have to become a different person to be truly understood.

The key insight: belonging cannot be manufactured by spending more time around people. It is built through specific acts of honest disclosure and reciprocal vulnerability. The feeling of being known is a product of choosing to be seen, which is a different action entirely from being present.

04 Esteem Needs

Maslow divided esteem into two categories: the esteem we derive from others (recognition, status, reputation) and the esteem we hold independently of others (self-respect, competence, integrity). The second type is structurally more stable than the first. External esteem is subject to conditions outside your control. Internal esteem is not.

The failure mode specific to high achievers is building an impressive structure of external esteem while the internal foundation is weak or hollow. This produces the imposter syndrome pattern: visible success coexisting with a private conviction that the success is undeserved, fragile, or fraudulent. The external evidence is plentiful. The internal register is not updated.

What unmet esteem needs look like in real life

Needing repeated reassurance that your work is good enough. Attributing success to luck or circumstance and failure to your own character. Avoiding situations where you might be exposed as less competent than you appear. A persistent gap between the confidence you project and the confidence you actually hold.

The key insight: esteem is not built by accumulating achievements. It is built by developing a consistent, honest relationship with your own competence: seeing clearly what you can do, where you are growing, and where you genuinely fall short. The person who can hold all three without distortion has solid esteem. Achievement alone does not get you there.

05 Self-Actualisation

Self-actualisation is Maslow's most contested concept and his most interesting one. It describes the drive to become fully what one is capable of becoming: not a fixed destination, but a direction. It is the experience of living in accordance with your deepest values and capacities, of growing toward rather than away from who you actually are.

The important structural point is that self-actualisation is genuinely difficult to access when lower levels are significantly depleted. This is not an excuse for deferring it indefinitely. It is a structural observation: trying to build purpose while safety needs are unmet is like trying to paint a wall that is still wet. The work does not hold.

What unmet self-actualisation needs look like in real life

A persistent sense that your life is not quite your own. Doing work that pays well or earns respect but feels fundamentally hollow. The specific dissatisfaction of being good at things that do not actually matter to you. A gap between who you are and who you sense you could be, with no clear path between them.

The key insight: self-actualisation is not a reward for completing the lower levels. It is a direction you can begin moving in at any point. What the lower levels provide is stability: the more solid the foundation, the more freely you can grow. Begin where you are. Build the foundation as you go.

D-Needs and B-Needs

Maslow distinguished between two fundamentally different types of motivation operating within the hierarchy.

Deficiency Needs (D-Needs)

Levels 1 through 4. These are needs that arise from a lack and are satisfied by filling that lack. When a D-need is met, the motivation to pursue it reduces. They are essentially maintenance needs: the goal is adequacy, not abundance. You do not need to maximise them. You need them sufficiently met to free attention for higher levels.

Being Needs (B-Needs)

Level 5 and beyond. These are needs that grow stronger when they are engaged with, not weaker. The more you pursue self-actualisation, the more you want it. This is why purpose-driven people often describe feeling more alive, not more satisfied. B-needs are not problems to be solved. They are directions to be followed.

An honest note on the model's limitations

Maslow's hierarchy has been criticised, fairly, for several things. The strict sequential ordering (you must satisfy level N before level N+1 becomes relevant) is not supported by the evidence: people pursue belonging and purpose even under conditions of significant physiological and safety deprivation. The hierarchy is better understood as a map of relative influence than a strict sequence. Additionally, Maslow developed his theory primarily through the study of exceptional individuals in a specific cultural context. The universal applicability of the model is not established. Use it as a lens that sharpens certain things, not as a complete description of human motivation.
